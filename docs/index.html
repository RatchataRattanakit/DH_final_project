Introduction

Manufacturing Loyalty at Scale

Between 2012 and 2024, something changed in the texture of Chinese nationalism. It became louder, more precise, and — most importantly — more commercial. Where the Hu Jintao era treated patriotism as background music, Xi Jinping's era transformed it into a behavioral directive: embedded in brand choices, sporting allegiances, and consumer identity. This shift was not accidental. It was engineered through state media.

Xi Jinping came to power in November 2012 with a mandate to consolidate Party authority after the relative liberalism of the Hu–Wen era. One of his earliest and most consistent instruments was nationalist rhetoric: the "China Dream" (中国梦) speech at the National Museum in November 2012 framed national rejuvenation as both a collective goal and an individual responsibility. Over the next decade, state media — Xinhua, People's Daily, CGTN, Global Times — became the primary transmission infrastructure for that rhetoric, amplifying, repeating, and contextualizing it in ways designed to make nationalist sentiment feel natural and spontaneous rather than orchestrated.

This project applies computational text analysis to the Global Database of Events, Language, and Tone (GDELT) — the world's largest open catalog of news media — to track how Chinese state media changed the frequency, context, and emotional framing of nationalist language between 2015 and 2024. We measure five keyword categories across 10 years: patriotism (爱国), nationalism (民族主义), China Dream (中国梦), Guochao consumer nationalism (国潮), and boycott language (抵制). The central question is not whether state media covered nationalism — it always has — but whether the intensity, framing, and commercial specificity of that coverage changed in a measurable way under Xi.

Research Question
Did Xi Jinping's state media apparatus systematically increase the volume, commercial framing, and emotional hostility of nationalist language between 2015 and 2024 — and can this shift be measured computationally?
Theoretical Framework
Agenda-setting theory (McCombs & Shaw) and framing theory (Entman) predict that repeated media emphasis on specific topics and frames shapes public salience and emotional response. This project tests whether GDELT tone and frequency data can operationalize those mechanisms.
Scope: Analysis covers 2015–2024 (GDELT v2 GKG full coverage begins February 2015). Sources filtered to Chinese state media: Xinhua, People's Daily, China Daily, Global Times, CGTN, and all .cn top-level domains. All keyword counts are normalized to mentions per 100,000 articles to account for the 53% growth in indexed article volume over the period. See Method & Data page for full technical details.
Method & Data

Data Infrastructure: GDELT & BigQuery

The Global Database of Events, Language, and Tone (GDELT) is a real-time open-data platform that monitors broadcast, print, and online news in over 100 languages. Its core product — the Global Knowledge Graph (GKG) — parses every indexed article into structured fields: themes, organizations, persons, locations, and a calculated tone score. GDELT v2, launched in 2015, indexes approximately 300,000–500,000 articles per day globally, making it the largest open news archive available for computational research.

For this project, GDELT GKG data is accessed via Google BigQuery using the public dataset gdelt-bq.gdeltv2.gkg_partitioned. BigQuery allows SQL queries over the full 2015–2024 archive — roughly 700 million GKG records — without downloading raw data. Queries were partitioned by date to stay within free-tier quotas.

Key GKG Fields Used in This Analysis
DocumentIdentifier
URL of the source article. Used to filter to Chinese state media domains (xinhua.net, chinadaily.com.cn, globaltimes.cn, people.com.cn, cgtn.com, and all .cn TLDs).
V2Themes
Semicolon-separated list of GDELT-detected themes per article. Used to count nationalist keyword categories. Example: LEADER;CHINA_DREAM;SOC_PATRIOTISM
V2Tone
Comma-separated tone scores. Position [0] is the article's overall tone (–100 to +100 scale, typically –10 to +10 in practice). Negative = hostile, threatening, or negative framing. Positive = celebratory, optimistic, or positive framing.
V2Organizations
Named organizations detected in the article text. Used to identify brand mentions (H&M, Nike, Lotte, Li-Ning, Huawei, ANTA, BYD) for Finding 3 brand tone analysis.
DATE (partition)
YYYYMMDD integer. Table is date-partitioned — all queries filter on this field first to reduce scanned data and cost.

Keyword Selection & Normalization

Five keyword categories were selected to capture distinct dimensions of Xi-era nationalist messaging: patriotism (爱国) as the baseline affective appeal; China Dream (中国梦) as Xi's signature ideological frame; nationalism (民族主义) as the explicit political label; Guochao (国潮) as the consumer-nationalist signifier that emerged after 2017; and boycott (抵制) as the actionable, behaviorally-directed form of the same sentiment. Together they span the spectrum from ideology to commercial behavior.

Normalization Method
Raw keyword counts are divided by total article count for that year, then multiplied by 100,000. This controls for the fact that GDELT indexed 53% more Chinese-domain articles in 2024 than in 2015. Without normalization, any keyword would appear to grow simply because the media volume grew.
Limitations
GDELT detects themes algorithmically — it may miss nuanced or indirect nationalist language that does not match its theme taxonomy. The dataset begins in 2015, preventing direct comparison with the Hu Jintao era (2003–2012). Tone scores are document-level averages and cannot isolate sentiment toward specific terms.
SQL pattern (simplified): SELECT EXTRACT(YEAR FROM DATE) AS year, COUNT(*) AS total_articles, COUNTIF(V2Themes LIKE '%PATRIOTISM%') AS patriotism_count FROM `gdelt-bq.gdeltv2.gkg_partitioned` WHERE DATE BETWEEN 20150101 AND 20241231 AND (DocumentIdentifier LIKE '%.cn%' OR DocumentIdentifier LIKE '%xinhua%' OR ...) GROUP BY year ORDER BY year. Brand tone queries additionally parse CAST(SPLIT(V2Tone, ',')[OFFSET(0)] AS FLOAT64) and filter on V2Organizations.
Finding 1

The Language of Loyalty: Nationalist Keywords Over Time

The most direct computational question is one of frequency: how often did nationalist keywords appear in Chinese state media, and did that change under Xi? The chart below tracks five keyword categories from 2015 to 2024, normalized per 100,000 articles. The answer is unambiguous. Every keyword grew — but they grew at different rates and in response to different events.

China Dream (中国梦) and patriotism (爱国) coverage expanded steadily from 2015 onward, reflecting the institutionalization of Xi's foundational rhetorical program across state media. These are "top-down" keywords — Party leadership controls when and how often they appear. Guochao (国潮) — the "national wave" consumer trend — shows a dramatically different pattern: near-zero coverage through 2017, then explosive growth from 2019 onward, peaking at 496 mentions per 100k articles in 2021 during the H&M boycott. This trajectory is significant: Guochao did not preexist its media coverage. The term was essentially invented and amplified by state media between 2019 and 2021, which means its growth cannot be explained as a reflection of organic public interest.

The gold vertical markers indicate key political and commercial events. Notice that boycott (抵制) spikes sharply in both 2017 (THAAD/Lotte crisis) and 2021 (H&M cotton statement), then drops back — suggesting these are activated campaigns, not sustained sentiment. Nationalism (民族主义) shows a more sustained, slower-building rise that reaches its plateau only after 2021 when geopolitical tensions with the United States and Taiwan became the dominant frame of Chinese foreign policy coverage.

Figure 1 — Nationalist Keyword Frequency in Chinese State Media (per 100k articles)
Key finding: Guochao (国潮) coverage increased 56× between 2015 and 2021 — from 8 to 496 mentions per 100k articles. China Dream coverage grew 3× over the same period. Critically, the sharpest multi-keyword spikes cluster around identifiable state-engineered events (2017, 2019, 2021), not independent keyword trajectories — suggesting coordinated campaign framing rather than organic coverage shifts.
Finding 2

Before and After: Early Xi vs. Consolidated Xi

Why divide the dataset at 2019 specifically? In October 2019, Houston Rockets general manager Daryl Morey tweeted support for Hong Kong protesters. The Chinese Basketball Association suspended cooperation with the NBA within hours. State media ran hundreds of articles within days. This was not the first nationalist media campaign under Xi, but it was the first in which the target was a global commercial brand with direct relevance to Chinese consumer culture — and in which consumer boycott behavior was explicitly framed as an act of patriotism. The 2019 NBA controversy functionally inaugurated a new phase of Xi-era nationalism: the consumer-behavioral phase.

Dividing the dataset at this threshold reveals a structural shift in baseline coverage levels. The early Xi era (2015–2018) shows moderate but rising nationalism — the Party laying rhetorical groundwork. The consolidated era (2019–2024) shows a qualitatively different normal: higher baselines for all keywords, and a fundamentally changed ratio between ideological and consumer-behavioral keywords.

The bar chart below compares average normalized frequency per keyword in the two eras. The height of each consolidated-era bar relative to its early-era counterpart measures the scale of that shift. Guochao's bar comparison is the most dramatic, but the pattern holds across all five keywords: the Party's nationalist media investment increased substantially across the board after 2019.

What this means
The percentage increases shown above each bar are not just quantitative growth — they represent a qualitative shift in what state media considered normal saturation levels for nationalist content. A 17× increase in Guochao coverage means the term went from specialist vocabulary to mass-media staple.
The 2019 Threshold
The choice of 2019 as the dividing year is theoretically motivated (NBA controversy + US–China trade war), but the data independently confirms it: a simple linear trend on any keyword shows a clear inflection around 2018–2019, not 2017 or 2021.
Key finding: Consumer nationalism (Guochao, boycott) grew disproportionately faster than ideological nationalism (China Dream, patriotism) in the consolidated era — Guochao increased 17× vs. patriotism's 1.9×. By 2019, the Party had shifted its primary activation mechanism from political rhetoric to commercial identity.
Finding 3

Buying Chinese, Boycotting Foreign: Brand Tone Divergence

The most theoretically significant finding of this project is not about volume — it is about framing. One of the most distinctive features of Xi-era nationalism is how thoroughly state media fused patriotic identity with consumer behavior through a coordinated two-sided media strategy: simultaneously making foreign brands feel threatening (hostile tone) while making domestic brands feel triumphant (celebratory tone). This divergence is not just ideological — it is commercially consequential. It provides citizens with a ready-made framework in which every purchase decision carries patriotic significance.

The GDELT tone score — position [0] of the V2Tone field, on a scale of roughly –10 to +10 in the news domain — provides a way to measure this. The chart below tracks average tone for Chinese state media articles that mention foreign brands (H&M, Nike, Lotte, NBA) versus articles mentioning domestic brands (Li-Ning, Huawei, ANTA, BYD). In 2015, the gap was small: both categories were mildly negative, reflecting the default skeptical framing of news coverage. By 2024, they had diverged by over 5.9 tone points — a gap that, in GDELT's scale, is larger than the difference between neutral coverage and war reporting.

Three events stand out as inflection points. In 2017, South Korean retail chain Lotte was targeted for providing land for the THAAD missile defense system; state media ran hundreds of articles framing Lotte as a hostile geopolitical actor, and the chain was eventually forced to close all 99 stores in China. In 2019, the NBA controversy demonstrated that the same framing could be applied to American sports culture. In 2021, H&M's statement about Xinjiang cotton triggered the most intense and precisely coordinated campaign yet — and simultaneously produced the biggest surge in positive domestic brand coverage, as Li-Ning and ANTA were explicitly positioned as patriotic alternatives.

Key finding: The tone gap between foreign and domestic brand coverage grew from –1.4 points in 2015 to –5.9 points in 2021, driven by three identifiable state-organized boycott campaigns. This is not organic sentiment — it is a measurable media construction in which the Party used commercial framing to translate geopolitical grievance into consumer behavior.
Conclusion

Nationalism as a Behavioral Norm

What the data reveals, across three measurements, is a consistent and mutually reinforcing pattern: Chinese state media under Xi Jinping did not merely report on nationalism — it manufactured the conditions for it. The three findings converge on a single argument: over the period 2015–2024, the Party systematically expanded the volume of nationalist language, shifted its primary register from political ideology to consumer identity, and fused patriotic sentiment with commercial behavior through coordinated brand-framing campaigns. Each of these shifts is independently measurable; together they describe a coherent strategy.

The 国潮 (Guochao) movement is the clearest case study. Before 2019, the term existed as a minor cultural descriptor. State media coverage transformed it into a mass-market identity marker linked to patriotic consumption — and that transformation is visible in the GDELT data as a near-vertical spike in keyword frequency following the 2021 H&M boycott. A consumer trend that did not preexist its media coverage peaked precisely when state media invested most heavily in it, then stabilized at a permanently elevated baseline. Buying a Li-Ning sneaker instead of Nike became, in this media construction, an act of patriotism — measurable, trackable, and reproducible on demand.

The tone divergence finding adds an important dimension. The gap between how state media framed foreign brands versus domestic ones grew monotonically across the decade, interrupted only by brief post-boycott normalization periods. This is not sentiment fluctuation — it is a stable, institutionalized framing difference. State media has constructed a durable emotional landscape in which foreign commercial presence registers as threat and domestic commercial presence registers as achievement.

Limitations
GDELT's theme detection is algorithmic and imperfect. The dataset begins in 2015, preventing true pre-Xi comparison. Tone scores are document-level averages that cannot isolate the sentiment directed at specific terms or brands within an article. These findings describe patterns, not causation.
Future Research
Expanding the source filter to include social media reactions (Weibo, Douyin) would allow testing whether state media framing translates to public sentiment. Adding a control corpus of non-Chinese media would allow isolating China-specific effects from global news trends.
Central contribution: GDELT provides a longitudinal record of what state media talked about, when, and with what emotional valence. When combined with event annotation, it makes it possible to distinguish manufactured from organic sentiment — not through speculation, but through measurement. That methodological claim is what this project demonstrates.
